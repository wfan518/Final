01

The Challenge

Heat is becoming an increasingly significant public health challenge in the Netherlands. Over the past 30 years, the average temperature has risen by approximately 1.1°C, while the number of tropical days (above 30°C) has risen from around 2 to 5 per year

Climate projections suggest this could increase to as many as 35 tropical days annually by the end of the century. The health consequences are already visible, with an estimated 250 heat-related deaths occurring in the Netherlands each year.

As climate change intensifies, both the frequency and severity of heat-related health impacts are expected to increase. Preparing for these challenges requires targeted, informed action from policymakers at every level.

02

Heat Risk Is Not Equal

Preparing for these challenges requires targeted and informed action. However, heat risk is not distributed equally across the population. Certain groups, such as elderly and children are physiologically more vulnerable to extreme temperatures, however people with fragile health and those experiencing chronic stress also face more barriers when protecting themselves. Additionally social and economic factors also play crucial roles in determining who is most at risk. For example low incomes can limit access to cooling, while social isolation reduces the likelihood of receiving support during heat alerts. This is particularly concerning given that 98% of heat-related deaths occur at home with the majority living only by themselves (Public Health Ontario, 2023).

All these different factors should be contemplated by municipal policymakers. However, due to the complex nature of heat vulnerability, this is not an easy feat. To make this information more digestible, this tool examines heat vulnerability through four main categories.

03

Four Dimensions of Vulnerability

Each theme captures a distinct aspect of heat vulnerability. Together they form a complete picture of which neighbourhoods face the greatest risk and why.

Theme 01
🌡️
Heat Exposure

Heat exposure refers to the intensity and duration of heat experienced by residents. Dense urban areas with asphalt, concrete, and buildings absorb and retain heat throughout the day, creating the urban heat island effect. Areas with limited vegetation also cool down more slowly at night, meaning residents in highly urbanised neighbourhoods often face prolonged heat stress.

Theme 02
👥
Vulnerable Populations

Some groups are physiologically more susceptible to the health impacts of heat. Older adults are less able to regulate body temperature and more likely to have underlying health conditions. Young children heat up more quickly and depend on caregivers to recognise distress. Individuals with chronic illness or mobility limitations face additional challenges. Neighbourhoods with larger concentrations of these residents are more likely to experience adverse health outcomes during extreme heat events.

Theme 03
💶
Socioeconomic Conditions

Socioeconomic circumstances strongly influence a person's ability to prepare for and respond to heat. Lower income households may be less able to afford cooling measures, energy costs, or home improvements that reduce indoor temperatures. Social isolation can also increase risk, as people living alone may have fewer opportunities to seek assistance or receive welfare checks during extreme heat. Communities experiencing higher levels of deprivation often have fewer resources available to adapt to increasing temperatures, making them more vulnerable to heat related health impacts.

Theme 04
🏗️
Built Environment & Access

The characteristics of the built environment can either increase or reduce vulnerability to heat. Older or poorly insulated housing may trap heat indoors, creating dangerous conditions even after outdoor temperatures begin to fall. Access to green spaces, shaded areas, cooling centres and other public services can help residents manage heat stress and recover from heat related illness. Conversely, neighbourhoods lacking these protective features may face greater health risks during prolonged periods of extreme heat.
