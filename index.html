The research context

Three pathways from
climate change to health harm

Heat waves are the deadliest weather event in the Netherlands. But the risk is not random — it follows deep structural patterns that policy can address.

01

Heat exposure is spatially unequal

Dense urban neighborhoods with little vegetation and high shares of dark surfaces experience Physiological Equivalent Temperatures up to 8°C higher than surrounding areas. Green space, parks, and water reduce this — but are unequally distributed across the country.

↑ 8°C PET gap between most and least exposed neighborhoods in major cities
02

The most vulnerable cannot adapt alone

Elderly residents, young children, people with frail health, and those experiencing chronic stress or loneliness face compounding barriers to self-protection. Low incomes mean no access to cooling, while social isolation means no one to check in during heat alerts.

↑ 3× higher hospitalization rate for residents aged 75+ during heat waves
03

Poor housing traps people inside with heat

Neighborhoods dominated by pre-2010 housing — built before modern thermal insulation standards — become indoor heat traps during prolonged heat events. Rental-heavy neighborhoods offer residents little control over retrofitting or installing cooling.

↑ Pre-2010 buildings show highest rates of inability to find indoor cooling
