The research context

Why heat-health risk is hard to act on

Heat-health vulnerability is not only produced by exposure, population sensitivity, and housing conditions. It also depends on whether evidence can be translated into municipal priorities, routines, and intervention timing.

01

Heat exposure is spatially unequal

Dense urban neighbourhoods with little vegetation and high shares of dark surfaces experience substantially higher heat stress than surrounding areas.

Urban heat is distributed unevenly across neighbourhoods
02

Vulnerability is socially patterned

Elderly residents, young children, people with frail health, and low-income households face compounding barriers to self-protection during heat events.

Heat risk increases where exposure and social vulnerability overlap
03

Housing and infrastructure shape indoor risk

Older housing, limited green space, poor cooling access, and rental constraints can turn outdoor heat exposure into a local public-health problem.

Built environments can reduce or amplify heat-health risk
04
Policy interpretation gap

Heat-health risk competes for policy attention

Dutch climate adaptation has long been shaped by collective experience with water and flood protection. Heat-health risks are newer, less visible, and harder to fit into established municipal routines.

The challenge is not only data, but interpretation
